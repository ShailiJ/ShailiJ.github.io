Sentiment Analysis of Alexandria Articles (ML1)

   For this part we used a python natural language processing api to process several articles from alexandria to understand the differences between the two, and how traditional NLP tools can be modified and adapted for financial analysis purposes

Sentiment Analysis using TextBlob and Comparison with Alexandria’s Scores

   TextBlob is a Python (2 and 3) library for processing textual data. It provides a simple API for diving into common natural language processing (NLP) tasks such as part-of-speech tagging, noun phrase extraction, sentiment analysis, classification, translation, and more.

   The first article that we experimented with is “Police Arrest 5 for Allegedly Jumping Deputy at Disney World”, this article deals with exclusively non-financial data. And the TextBlob’s sentiment analysis yields a sentiment score of 0.02 which is inline with Alexandria’s 0. It seems that ACT has similar performance with normal NLP program in the face of a nonfinancial article.

   The second article is “2 sentenced in Coke trade secret case” which deals with two publicly traded companies, Coca-cola and pepsi. In this case, the sentiment analysis of TextBlob failed, yielding a sentiment score of 0.12 vesus a -1 by ACT. If we look into the text of the file, no emotional words are used, just factual statements. The lack of emotional words led TextBlob to give this article a neutral score, but ACT managed to capture the negative impact that event has on the relevant companies.

   The third article is “Borse Dubai and NASDAQ Have Secured 47.6 Per Cent of OMX Shares Through Direct Ownership, Option Agreements and Irrevocable Undertakings”, the TextBlob Algorithm failed to capture the intrinsic positive effect of such transaction, yielding a sentiment score of 0 while the ACTA yields a one. Here, the words that would be neutral in the normal context actually should be interpreted as positive.

Conclusion:

   Overall, the regular sentiment analysis does a good job interpreting events without hidden financial connotation, however, if financial terms and events are involved. It is fairly difficult for a standard NLP algorithm to detect the sentiment.

Alexandria’s Sentiment Analysis Algorithm

   By contrast, ACTA’s sentiment classification engine offers key benefits:

  1. ACTA applies its own probabilistic algorithms to sentiment assessment – employing the patterns it has discovered through a statistical methodology – rather than be bound by rules previously fixed by humans. This allows ACTA to apply a far more complex “understanding” of the content it reads, including “unknown” rules and relationships that other systems can’t identify.
  2. ACTA dynamically selects and weights a virtually infinite range of classification patterns based on the strength of relationships, not due to any pre-assigned weighting.
  3. ACTA identifies sentiment within a broader context of meaning – given that the whole of a given article is taken in by the engine and analyzed as opposed to any individual words found within it.
